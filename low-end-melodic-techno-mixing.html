Low-End in Melodic Techno: The Techniques That Actually Work

Sub bass management in Melodic Techno is an art form. The techniques that translate from your studio to the dancefloor.

The low end in Melodic Techno is the foundation of everything. Get it right and the track breathes, moves, and hits differently on a real soundsystem. Get it wrong and you have a track that sounds fine in headphones and falls apart at 103 dB on a dancefloor with real subwoofers.

Having mixed and mastered hundreds of electronic tracks from my studio in Milan — across Melodic Techno, Afro House, Deep House, and Melodic House — here are the techniques that consistently produce results that translate.

Understanding Why Melodic Techno Low-End Is Different

Melodic Techno is not just Techno with a melody. It is a genre with a specific emotional and physical intention: it must create physical sensation through bass frequency, while simultaneously supporting atmospheric and harmonic content in the mids and highs. This creates a specific mixing challenge that does not exist in simpler electronic genres.

The kick and bass in Melodic Techno do not just keep time. They carry emotional weight. A sub that lacks definition muddies the atmosphere. A kick that is too present kills the hypnotic quality. The balance is delicate, and it requires a different approach than Hip Hop, RnB, or even straight Techno.

Technique 1: Sub Allocation and Frequency Ownership

Every element in your mix needs a defined frequency range. In the low end, this means deciding very clearly: who owns below 60 Hz? Who owns 60-100 Hz? Who owns 100-200 Hz?

In most Melodic Techno productions, the architecture works as follows:

  • Below 40 Hz: sub bass fundamental — this is felt more than heard, and it should be clean and focused, not muddy
  • 40-80 Hz: the "body" of the bass — where the groove lives and where the sub makes its most physical impact on the listener
  • 80-120 Hz: shared territory between kick body and bass upper harmonics — this is where most low-end clashes happen and where careful EQ decisions are required
  • 120-200 Hz: kick body and warmth — this range determines whether your track sounds "full" or "thin" on smaller systems

Use high-pass filters aggressively on everything that should not be contributing to the low end. Synth pads, atmospheric textures, percussive elements — filter these hard below the frequency where they are actually contributing musical information. Every element with unnecessary low-frequency content is competing with your kick and bass and blurring the definition you need.

Technique 2: Sidechain as Composition, Not Just Mixing

Sidechaining in Melodic Techno is not a corrective tool — it is a compositional one. The pumping interaction between kick and bass is part of the genre's identity. But the way most producers set it up produces a mechanical, obvious effect rather than the organic, breathing quality of the best productions in the genre.

Key parameters to focus on:

Attack: a very fast attack (0.1-1 ms) preserves the initial transient of the kick before the compression engages. This is what makes the kick feel "in front of" the bass rather than the two elements competing. A slow attack causes the kick to disappear into the bass on impact.

Release: this controls the "pump." In Melodic Techno, a release time tuned to the tempo (try 100-150 ms at 128 BPM as a starting point) creates a movement that feels musical rather than mechanical. Listen to how the bass swells back in after the kick — that swell should feel like part of the groove, not an artifact.

Ratio: avoid extreme ratios (8:1 or higher) for sub content. 3:1 to 4:1 with appropriate threshold placement typically sounds more natural and more powerful than heavy compression with a high ratio.

Technique 3: Mono Below 120 Hz

This is non-negotiable for dancefloor playback. Everything below approximately 120 Hz should be mono, or very close to mono. Stereo low-frequency content causes phase cancellation issues on soundsystems where left and right speakers are not perfectly aligned — which is virtually every real club and festival installation.

Check your sub and kick regularly in mono. If the kick disappears or significantly loses impact when you sum to mono, you have a phase problem that needs to be addressed before the mix reaches mastering.

Width in Melodic Techno comes from the mids and highs — from the atmospheric pads, the melodic content, the spatial processing on percussion. Not from the low end.

Technique 4: Harmonic Enhancement for Translation

Sub bass below 40-50 Hz does not reproduce on laptop speakers, earbuds, or small monitors. Yet a track should still feel "full" on these systems. The solution is harmonic enhancement: adding controlled harmonic content in the 80-200 Hz range that suggests the presence of the sub even when the fundamental is not reproduced.

This can be achieved with saturation on the bass channel, with parallel distortion, or with a dedicated harmonic exciter. The goal is subtle — you are not distorting the bass, you are adding harmonics that help smaller systems represent the low-end energy you intend.

Test your low-end translation regularly: after you set your levels on your studio monitors, check on headphones, then on your phone speaker, then on a small Bluetooth speaker. The relative impact of kick and bass should be consistent across all three, even if the absolute frequency content is not.

Technique 5: Afro House and Melodic House — Where the Rules Differ

Afro House has a different low-end philosophy than Melodic Techno. The bass in Afro House is more melodic, more present in the upper harmonics, and more rhythmically complex. The relationship between bass and percussion is looser and more conversational.

In practice, this means sidechaining is often less aggressive, the bass sits higher in the frequency balance, and there is more acceptable movement in the 80-200 Hz range. The groove lives in the interaction between bass, kick, and percussion rather than in the mechanical precision of sidechain timing.

Melodic House sits between these two worlds: the atmospheric quality of Melodic Techno, the groove and warmth of Afro House. Low-end decisions in Melodic House should serve the emotional quality of the track first, and the dancefloor physics second.

The Mastering Stage: What Changes and What Does Not

All the decisions above happen in the mix. By the time your track reaches mastering, the low-end relationships between elements should be set. Mastering can refine the overall low-frequency balance — add controlled low-shelf boost, tighten the sub with dynamic EQ, adjust the kick-bass interaction with multiband processing — but it cannot fix fundamental problems in the mix.
